Our Mission Statement

This is more than fiction.

We strive to create some of the most diverse and highest quality virtual media available and to foster a supportive platform for writers of all skill levels and backgrounds to showcase their works for eager readers’ pleasure. We aim to nurture a community that respects what makes each of us unique, understands social injustices in relation to media and representation, and that will provide constructive criticism and encouragement to all our members. We want to help transform entertainment media to be more inclusive of the under-represented and conscious of the diversity of the audience it reaches, one step at a time.

At FCVNtv, our executive team is made up completely of women who also intersect with various other under-represented groups. This gives us a unique angle in which to do our part in tackling the very real issue that continues to plague our media: a lack of diversity and representation for minorities.

As it stands, white heterosexual men still dominate our screens no matter what we’re watching: film, television, or a streaming service. According to the Women’s Media Center’s report, "The Status Of Women in the U.S. Media 2017", only 33.5% of named or speaking characters were women, only 26.3% were people of colors and in a study of 4,370 speaking characters in the top 800 movies from 2007 to 2015, only 32 of them identified as LGBT.

It’s also important to note that the diversity and representation issues don’t end on screen. In the San Diego State University’s Center for the Study of Women in Television and Film’s "The Celluloid Ceiling" study, it was found that in the to 250 films of 2015-2016, we learn that behind-the-scenes is even more abysmal. Of all the directors, writers, producers, executive producers, editors, and cinematographers on those films, only a pitiful 17% were women. A study by the Directors Guild of America, "Feature Film Diversity Report", found that in the feature films opening in 2013 and 2014, 12.5% of directors were people of color, only 1.3% of which were women. In 2015, a mere 5.3% of writers for theatrical films were people of color.

The need for change is glaringly obvious and the fight to bring more diversity to media of all kinds is an ongoing one. It's important that we, whether we write for fun or we write as potential, aspiring screenwriters of film and television, acknowledge our importance in contributing to the movement.

We push for our content to embody these goals in any way that we can and we hope that you will join us.
